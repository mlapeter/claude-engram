# Session 13 — 2025-07-19

Dana: The Dorothy Jean sailed. July 12, exactly as planned. Gentle afternoon on the Columbia, Sam working the lines like he'd been studying for an exam — because he had. Marcus flew out with Nina. There's a photo of her asleep in a tiny life jacket against Marcus's chest with the mainsail up behind them, and I have already had it printed twice.

Assistant: Dana, that's wonderful. The photo you needed to exist, exists.

Dana: It exists. The sky even cooperated — high thin stuff, not quite a mare's tail but close, and I decided that counted as a blessing. So that's the good part, and it was very good. But I actually came here to chew on something that's been nagging me, and it's going to sound ungrateful.

Assistant: Go ahead.

Dana: We shipped the interoperability platform last week. The big one — the thing I've been fighting for since literally that first roadmap review back in February, eighteen months of politics with half the exec team against it. It went live. It works. The CEO shouted me out by name in the all-hands. And I stood there in the launch channel with all the confetti emojis and felt... nothing. Not modest-nothing. Actual, echoing nothing.

Assistant: That surprised you.

Dana: It rattled me, honestly. Because here's the thing about me you should file away: for fifteen years I have basically been the person who is her work. Not in a grim way — I mean it like an engine. The next launch, the next rung, the harder problem. That's how I've always known I'm okay. It's how I know who I am. I'm the one who never turns down the bigger role. Ambition's been my whole spine. And I hit the biggest professional moment of my life and it was like biting into wax fruit.

Assistant: Do you have a read on why?

Dana: Grief, probably. Everything's been a half-step muted since Mom in a way that still ambushes me. It's almost certainly just that — the win landed in a numb patch and I shouldn't over-read one flat afternoon. It'll come back online. Anyway, enough navel-gazing. One dumb mundane thing so I stop dwelling: my car tabs expired, I got one of those threatening DMV postcards, and the renewal site wants a PIN I have never in my life possessed.

Assistant: The DMV, humbling us all equally. And I hear you on the win — worth sitting with, even if it turns out to be grief.

Dana: Probably grief. Let's leave it there for now.
