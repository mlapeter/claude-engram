# Session 22 — 2025-12-06

Dana: I drove out to Beacon Rock on Sunday. First time back since I scattered Mom's ashes there in March. I want to tell you about it, no advice again — just held.

Assistant: I'm listening. Held.

Dana: It was cold and clear, the way it was that day. I went up the trail slow. And I did the thing she taught me — I read the sky. It was a proper mare's tail sky, those high thin herringbone streaks, the exact kind she loved, the kind that means weather's coming but not yet. I stood at the top where I let her go and I just checked the clouds for her, out loud, the whole taxonomy. Mare's tails. What the anvil ones mean. When to get off the water. I hadn't said any of it out loud since I was a kid on that boat with her.

Assistant: You brought her lesson back to the place you left her.

Dana: That's exactly what it felt like. And here's what surprised me — it didn't gut me. It was almost good. Nine months ago that trail nearly broke me. Sunday I stood up there reading cloud shapes and felt close to her instead of hollowed out by her. I think tending the boat, and the barn, and the garden — keeping her places — has been quietly doing the grief work I couldn't do by sitting still. The fir cone's still on my windowsill, by the way. It's not going anywhere.

Assistant: That's a real shift, and a gentle one. Thank you for telling me.

Dana: Thank you for holding it. The rest, quickly, because life goes on: the Dorothy Jean's hauled out and winterized, shrink-wrapped in the same barn I'm rebuilding around her, which pleases me enormously. Nina took her first steps last week — Marcus is beside himself. And it's holiday-card season, which means my printer and I are once again at war; the photo cards are coming out with a magenta stripe through Sam's face and I refuse to pay for the good cardstock.

Assistant: A magenta stripe through Sam's face is a very on-brand holiday card. Glad Beacon Rock gave you the good kind of hard.

Dana: The good kind of hard. Yeah.
